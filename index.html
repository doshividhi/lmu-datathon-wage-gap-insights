Q1 Is there evidence of pay discrimination?
Our strategy was to build three progressively controlled regression models to isolate the effect of gender on wages.

Model 1 (Raw): Women earn −11.1% less with no controls. Model 2 (+ Human Capital): Gap grows to −14.1% — because women are MORE educated, controlling for education reveals a larger penalty. Model 3 ★ (Preferred, + Occupation & Industry FE): Even inside the same job and sector, women earn −10.2% less (p<0.001).

Q2 Does prior salary hurt women? Should the ban be implemented nationally?
In 2022, men earn ~$29.3/hr while women earn ~$25.1/hr — a ~14% gap. The relationship between previous wages and current wages shows a strong positive trend, meaning salary history anchors workers to prior pay levels. Because women historically start lower, this perpetuates inequality at every job transition.

After California introduced the salary history ban in 2018, women's wage growth increased to ~29% while male growth remained ~26–27% — a 2–3 percentage point improvement. Despite this, the overall 14% gap persists, showing historical disparities are not yet erased.

Recommendation: Implement the ban nationally. It directly attacks the anchoring mechanism. But it must be combined with broader pay equity policies.

Q3 Would the ban weaken the prior pay → current pay relationship?
After the salary history ban, we expect the relationship between prior pay and current pay to weaken — because employers can no longer directly rely on past salary when setting compensation.

However, we would not expect it to reach zero. Prior wages still reflect factors such as skills, experience, and education which continue to legitimately influence future earnings. The policy reduces salary anchoring, not all pay determinants.

✓ YES — The Link Will Weaken

  • Employers can no longer anchor starting salaries to prior pay
  • Pay must be justified by job requirements + market data
  • Women underpaid in prior roles can now negotiate from market value
  • CA ban empirical evidence shows reduced anchoring effect post-2018

✗ NO — It Won't Reach Zero

  • Skills, portfolio, and experience still signal productivity → still predict pay
  • Implicit bias in offer-making doesn't disappear with one question removed
  • Negotiation behavior differences persist (gender norms, social penalties)
  • Network effects and referrals still advantage incumbents (often male)

Direct Policy Recommendation
Implementing a national salary history ban could help reduce the persistence of historical wage disparities. The data suggests women's wage growth improved by approximately 2–3 percentage points after the policy period — consistent with the policy's goal of allowing wages to grow independently of past salary levels.

However, because a 14% wage gap still remains, this policy alone may not completely eliminate gender wage inequality. It should therefore be considered as one part of a broader set of policies aimed at improving pay equity.

"The data suggests that salary history can reinforce wage inequality, and while California's policy appears to improve women's wage growth by a few percentage points, broader policy efforts may be needed to fully close the remaining 14% gender wage gap."
